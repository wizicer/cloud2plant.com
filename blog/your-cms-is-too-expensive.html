“Your CMS is too expensive”

July 26, 2014

The other day, someone wrote in to say, “your CMS is too expensive.”

I asked them what they thought a fair price would be, but that was all I’d ever hear from them. After pondering this for awhile, I’ve come to the conclusion that they either misunderstood our pricing model or they wanted our service to be completely free.

The reason I disagree with their statement is because, when you do the math, a few dollars a month isn’t much when you bundle it up with the cost of domain registrations and web hosting. Maybe they’re not presenting it to their clients right. Maybe they think something like 10 cents a day is…a lot?

I have another theory.

In the past, I’ve talked to web designers and freelancers who let their clients maintain their own domains, hosting fees, etc. This is a bad idea. I know this because I’ve seen firsthand a number of clients unknowingly let their domains or web hosting expire. Sometimes they didn’t even know they were responsible for paying it. It’s hard to point fingers, even if you did inform them of the responsibility. It’s not their area of expertise—it’s yours.

My theory is that some people design-and-run. That is, they don’t build relationships with their clients. Instead, they get hired, do the job, get paid, and never interact with the client again. That’s really tragic, because building business relationships is critical to long-term success.

When you build relationships with your clients, not only will it open things up for additional business with them in the future, but they’ll consult with you and learn to trust your judgment as an expert in the field. They’ll listen to you and honor your recommendations. If you tell them to use a premium web host over a bulk host, they’ll agree. If you tell them to use a certain CMS to manage their content, they’ll oblige (this is especially true when the difference in costs are negligible). And if their experience with you is a good one, they’ll almost always tell their friends.

Maybe the problem here isn’t so much that our service is too expensive—we have many users that would argue otherwise. Maybe it’s that this particular person simply wasn’t into building relationships with his or her clients. Maybe they don’t want to be responsible for dealing with them after the job is done, in which case “free” would be a more attractive price for them.

I hope that’s not the case, but if it is, I hope that person realizes the cost of not building relationships with clients is much more than the couple dollars a month that we charge. It could cost them a lot of business in the long run.
